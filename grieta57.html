Grieta57

This sculptural sound installation explores cognitive and cultural practices on spaces, their evolution into places, and beyond-human relationships developed through geographic and historical association. By engaging space visually, physically and sonically, the work utilizes auditory and visual representational markers of places from the artist’s personal history to propose conscious perception as a means of agency in the fluid morphing of existence within clashing physical and mental environments.

The work is comprised of four sound objects made of steel pipes, with speakers of different dimensions attached to them as well as an Arduino based MIDI controller with four ultrasonic sensors that translate physical gestures into modulation sources for specific parameters within Ableton Live. Live environmental sound captured by a suspended microphone is processed in real time in parallel with a looped audio file which includes recordings of heavily detuned guitar strings and digitally created sample mashups of field and vocal recordings. Both sound sources are digitally mixed and morphed using a Spectral Morphing tool, with the degreee of morphing being determined by the signal input of one of the ultrasonic sensors in the MIDI interface.

Each sculptural sound object is assigned a specific channel within the Ableton Live project, where a convolution reverb unit is utilized to simulate the acoustic responses of four specific real spaces: The inside of Tejuino’s car, the towering Gallardo Family Mausoleum in the Mezquitán Cemetery in Guadalajara (not related to the artist’s own family), the dark and spiraling stairs in the building of a friend’s flat, and a a fourth unnamed space. The gestures captured by a second ultrasonic sensor make it so the sound navigates across the four different sculptural objects arranged in the space, and by extension, the digital representations of the different real places and their acoustic signatures. The third ultrasonic sensor allows for the reduction of the reverberation effect with a spectral reverb removal tool, introducing digital artifacts to the sound as it is deprived from its dimensional characteristics and forced back into the basic form of its substance. The fourth sensor controls a gate, which prevents the lower volume segments of the material from being played back by the sound system.
Each sculptural sound object plays back the fed sound with a specific set of tonal and textural conditions determined by its structural composition, the dimensions and angling of the attached speakers, the degree of amplification given and the additional characteristics of the digital emulation of a real space. The activation of the space where the work is installed, possessing its own acoustic characteristics, influences the perception of each of these elements, and creates a specific situated auditory experience.

The notion of shapeshifting and morphing between the textural, noise- infused looped audio and the live microphone feed serves as an analogy for the embodiment of environmental stimuli, the effects of experience in an individual’s own constitution and a reflexion on the ability to consciously discern the intricacies and nuances of this process.

By introducing the interface as a tool of agency, one becomes participant in the environment, and consciously develops the skill to interact with fractured, conflicting and dissonant facets of reality encountered in the inbetweenness of ideology, personality, identity and their geographic, historico-cultural and psychosomatic particularities.

Engaging the body in a conscious learning process with a volatile interactive system is intended to invite concentration on sensorymotor activity and the association processes triggered, going beyond binary judgments of error and precision, and revealing the value and extended possibilities of semiotic ill-fitting. A great deal of inspiration and the theoretical backdrop from this work comes from Gloria Anzaldúa’s Borderlands Consciousness concept as proposed in her 1987 anthropological and literary work Borderlands/La Frontera: The New Mestiza, as well as the research conducted for the BA thesis titled Reverberating languages: Space as a vessel for the construction of meaning networks through interaction with sound (2025).
